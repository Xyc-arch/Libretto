CONCEPT: Extended / altered harmony (9ths, 11ths, 13ths, altered dominants)
ID: J-EXT
FILE: jazz.txt
STATUS: jazz-idiom
ATTEST: 17/28 jazz songs
GENRE: jazz (bebop, swing, standards, fusion)
THEORY: chord extensions (9/11/13), altered dominants (b9/#9/#11/b13), upper-structure voicings
ROLE: harmonic color, tension, jazz sophistication
FEATURE: 5+-note stacked chords beyond the triad/7th
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
WHAT: Jazz harmony stacks past the triad — the 7th plus 9th/11th/13th, and on dominants the
  altered tensions (b9, #9, #11, b13). Voicings routinely sound 5–6 distinct pitch classes.
  This is the single biggest harmonic difference from pop/folk (which stay triadic/diatonic).
EXAMPLE: song_0132 (Thelonious Monk — Nice Work If You Can Get It) bar 5 — [Part1: C2+Eb3+A3+Bb3+D4+Eb4@1>2] over C: b7(Bb), 9(D), 13(A), #9(Eb) = a C13(#9) altered stack
EXAMPLE: song_0131 (Duke Ellington — Don't Get Around Much Anymore) bar 70 — [Piano: F3+G3+C#4+E4+B4@13>3] Fmaj7(E) + 9(G) + #11(B) extended voicing
COMPOSE: Stack beyond the triad. Add the 7th, then the 9th (root+2 semitones up an octave),
  11th/#11 (root+5/+6), and 13th (root+9). For an altered dominant, raise/lower the 5th and 9th
  (b9=root+1, #9=root+3, #11=root+6, b13=root+8). Put root + 7th low, pile the extensions on top
  (a rootless upper structure). Pairs with H-PALETTE / H-DOM7.
DOSAGE (jazz): har_chromaticism [0.07–0.23] (vs global 0.02–0.11); har_distinct_pc ~12 (use all 12);
  har_dimaug_rate [0.22–0.69] (vs global 0.03–0.34). Jazz lives at the high-chromatic end.

CONCEPT: ii–V–I and turnaround vocabulary
ID: J-IIVI
FILE: jazz.txt
STATUS: jazz-idiom
ATTEST: 15/28 jazz songs
GENRE: jazz (standards, bebop, swing)
THEORY: ii–V–I, functional root motion up a fourth, turnarounds (I–VI–ii–V), circle of fifths
ROLE: harmonic engine, cadence, sectional motion
FEATURE: bass roots up a 4th on successive chords
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
WHAT: The harmonic engine of jazz: a minor-7th (ii) → dominant-7th (V) → major-7th (I), the bass
  roots rising a perfect fourth each step. Turnarounds (I–VI–ii–V) recycle it to loop sections.
EXAMPLE: song_0132 (Thelonious Monk — Nice Work If You Can Get It) bars 78–80 — bass roots G→C→F (Gm7–C7–Fmaj7, ii–V–I in F)  [Part1: ... G2@3>1 ... ]
EXAMPLE: song_0138 (Gillespie Dizzy — A Night in Tunesia) bars 27–29 — bass roots A→D→G (ii–V–I)  [Part2: A2@1>3 Eb2@4>3 ...]
COMPOSE: Drive the harmony with up-a-fourth root motion: place ii (minor7) for a bar, V (dom7,
  often altered — see J-EXT) for a bar, I (maj7) to resolve; the bass roots are a fourth apart each
  step. End sections with a I–VI–ii–V turnaround back to the top. Build cadences this way rather than
  the pop IV–V–I. Pairs with H-FOURTHS, H-DOM7.
DOSAGE (jazz): har_fourth_motion_rate HIGH (this is the jazz engine — well above the pop band);
  har_root_motion_entropy moderate-high [~0.71–0.89] as roots cycle the circle of fifths.

CONCEPT: Swing / triplet feel
ID: J-SWING
FILE: jazz.txt
STATUS: jazz-idiom
ATTEST: 23/28 jazz songs
GENRE: jazz (swing, bebop, shuffle, ballad)
THEORY: swing eighths, triplet subdivision, 12/8 feel, long-short articulation
ROLE: rhythmic signature, feel, groove
FEATURE: triplet-grid bars (grid 12/6/24), swung eighths
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
WHAT: The rhythmic signature of jazz: beats divide in THREE (triplets), eighths are swung
  (long-short), not even. ~82% of the jazz corpus is predominantly triplet-grid — the single most
  jazz-discriminating rhythm axis (triplet_share splits jazz far above pop/funk/electronic).
EXAMPLE: song_0132 (Thelonious Monk — Nice Work If You Can Get It) — GRID 12t (adaptive), 25 triplet-grid bars
EXAMPLE: song_0143 (Dave Brubeck — Take Five) — GRID 12t (adaptive)
EXAMPLE: song_0131 (Duke Ellington — Don't Get Around Much Anymore) — GRID 12t (adaptive)
COMPOSE: Quantize to a triplet grid (grid 12 = 8th-note triplets; slot = 1/3 beat). Swing the
  eighths: on each beat play the first triplet-slot long and the third short (onsets at slot 1 and
  slot 3 of each beat's three slots, skipping slot 2) rather than even eighths. Use for any straight-
  ahead swing or shuffle; pair with J-WALK. See also R-TRIPLET.
DOSAGE (jazz): rhy_triplet_share [0.16–0.60] (vs global 0.02–0.29 — jazz is THE high-triplet genre);
  rhy_syncopation_rate [0.48–0.72].

CONCEPT: Bebop melodic vocabulary (gap-fill leaps + chromatic approach tones)
ID: J-BEBOP
FILE: jazz.txt
STATUS: jazz-idiom
ATTEST: 26/28 jazz songs
GENRE: jazz (bebop, hard-bop, swing)
THEORY: chromatic approach/enclosure, gap-fill (leap then step-back), wide-range running lines
ROLE: melodic line, soloing vocabulary, forward motion
FEATURE: scale-run + leaps filled by step + chromatic neighbor approaches
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
WHAT: Bebop melody = fast scalar runs punctuated by leaps that are immediately "filled" by step in
  the opposite direction (M-GAPFILL, attested in 26/28 jazz songs), and chord tones approached
  chromatically from a semitone away. Lines are wide-ranging and harmonically pointed.
EXAMPLE: song_0131 (Duke Ellington — Don't Get Around Much Anymore) bar 42 — [Trumpet: Eb5@1>3 E5+F5+F#5@3>2 G5+G#5+A5@4>2 Bb5+B5@5>2 C6@7>4] a fully chromatic ascending run (Eb-E-F-F#-G-G#-A-Bb-B-C)
EXAMPLE: song_0134 (George Benson — On Broadway) bar 53 — [Part7: C5@1>1 C5@2>1 G4@3>2 D5@5>1 ...] leap down then step back (gap-fill)
COMPOSE: Build lines mostly from scale steps, then drop in an expressive leap (6th/7th/octave) and
  answer it with a step in the other direction (M-GAPFILL). Approach target chord tones chromatically:
  land on a chord tone on the beat, preceded by the note a semitone below/above on the off-beat
  (chromatic enclosure). Keep the line wide (octave-plus). Pairs with M-GAPFILL, M-RANGE.
DOSAGE (jazz): mel_interval_entropy [0.74–0.85], mel_voice_range [18.5–53.5] st (wide),
  har_chromaticism [0.07–0.23].

CONCEPT: Comping (sparse syncopated chordal voicings)
ID: J-COMP
FILE: jazz.txt
STATUS: jazz-idiom
ATTEST: 21/28 jazz songs
GENRE: jazz (piano/guitar comping behind a soloist)
THEORY: comping, rootless voicings, syncopated chord stabs, "leaving space"
ROLE: accompaniment, harmonic support, rhythmic interplay
FEATURE: short off-beat chord hits, not sustained blocks
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
WHAT: Jazz accompaniment ("comping") plays short, rootless chord voicings as SYNCOPATED stabs on
  the off-beats, leaving space rather than sustaining block chords on the downbeat — a rhythmic
  conversation with the soloist.
EXAMPLE: song_0132 (Thelonious Monk — Nice Work If You Can Get It) bar 4 — [Part1: Eb3+B3+F4@3>1 F2+A3+Eb4@5>1 Eb3+B3+F4@8>4] chord stabs on off-beat slots (3,5,8), not on the downbeat
COMPOSE: Voice the chord WITHOUT the root (let the bass cover it) — typically 3rd + 7th + an
  extension — and play it as a SHORT stab (1–2 slots) on the off-beats (@3,@7,@11,@15 of a 16-slot
  bar, or the off-triplet in swing), varying which off-beats each bar so it anticipates the next
  chord. Leave bars/beats empty. The opposite of a sustained pad. Pairs with R-SYNCO.
DOSAGE (jazz): rhy_syncopation_rate [0.48–0.72]; tex_mean_simultaneity moderate (3-note rootless
  voicings); rhy_density_variability mid–high (space between stabs).

CONCEPT: Walking bass (quarter-note root + passing motion)
ID: J-WALK
FILE: jazz.txt
STATUS: jazz-idiom
ATTEST: 18/28 jazz songs
GENRE: jazz (swing, bebop, standards)
THEORY: walking bass, quarter-note pulse, scalar/chromatic passing tones, chord-tone targeting
ROLE: bass foundation, forward propulsion, harmonic outlining
FEATURE: a bass note on every beat moving by step/chromatic between chord roots
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
WHAT: The jazz bass foundation: a note on EVERY beat (quarter pulse), but unlike a static root-pulse
  it WALKS — chord root on the downbeat, then scale/chromatic passing tones leading by step into the
  next chord's root. Outlines the harmony while propelling the swing.
EXAMPLE: song_0137 (Oscar Peterson — Emily) bars 2–5 — [Part2: C2@1>2 Bb1@3>2 C2@5>2 D2@7>2 Eb2@9>2 | F2@1>3 G2@4>3 Bb2@7>3 C3@10>3] quarter pulse, stepwise passing (C–Bb–C–D–Eb / F–G–Bb–C)
EXAMPLE: song_0138 (Gillespie Dizzy — A Night in Tunesia) bars 27–30 — [Part2: A2@1>3 Eb2@4>3 ...] beat-by-beat bass
COMPOSE: Put a bass note on each beat (@1,@5,@9,@13 of a 4/4 bar, or each triplet beat in swing).
  On the chord's downbeat play its root; on the following beats walk by step or chromatic passing
  tone toward the NEXT chord's root, landing on it on the next downbeat (approach from a semitone
  above/below on beat 4). Use chord tones on strong beats, passing tones between. A walking cousin of
  R-ROOTPULSE — but moving, not static. Pairs with J-IIVI, J-SWING.
DOSAGE (jazz): rhy_onset_density_per_bar high (a note per beat); rhy_mean_dur_beats ~1 (quarters);
  the bass is the densest voice — distinct from a held pop root.
